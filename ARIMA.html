Weather forecasting¶

Authors: Piotr Pigo, Aleksandra Pestka

This script enables the user to forecast monthly average temperature,wind force,precipitation etc. benefiting by provided weather dataset. The chosen method is ARIMA - Autoregressive integrated moving average. Model requires three parameters (p,d,q) and is traditionally configured manually:

'p' is the order (number of time lags) of the autoregressive model)
'd' is the degree of differencing (the number of times the data have had past values subtracted)
'q' is the order of the moving-average model

The following data are findings from website: dane.imgw.pl (monthly open-source datasets for KRAKW-OBSERWATORIUM)

With this script we would like to forecast monthly temperature basing on datasets within the latest 11 full years (Jan 2007- Dec 2017).

In the next part we will develop ARIMA model (as described into the introduction). However, there should be provided stationary time series. Fortunately we can make it stationary by differencing the series and using a statistical test (Dickey-Fuller test) to confirm that the result is stationary. Mentioned test is a unit root test, which means that it determines how strongly a time series is defined by a trend.
It uses an autoregressive model and optimizes an information criterion across multiple different lag values.

Null Hypothesis (H0):
If failed to be rejected, it suggests the time series has a unit root, meaning it is non-stationary. It has some time dependent structure.
Alternate Hypothesis (H1):
The null hypothesis is rejected; it suggests the time series does not have a unit root, meaning it is stationary. It does not have time-dependent structure.

It is high time to interpret the p-value from the test. As long as p-value 0.05 we are allowed to reject the null hypothesis (H0),moreover, the data does not have a unit root and is stationary. Further, the more negative the ADF statistic is, the more likely we are to reject the null hypothesis.The ADF value of -3.73 is less than the value of -3.488 at 1%. This suggests that we can reject the null hypothesis with a significance level of less than 1%.

Statistical correlation summarizes the strength of the relationship between two variables. We can calculate the correlation for time series observations with observations with previous time steps, called lags.
The correlation of the time series observations is calculated with values of the same series at previous times and this is called autocorrelation.
The second subplot represents partial autocorrelation: "the partial autocorrelation at lag k is the correlation that results after removing the effect of any correlations due to the terms at shorter lags." (source: " Partial Autocorrelations, Introductory Time Series with R.")
Confidence intervals are drawn as a cone.

Based on the above subplots we can say that the ACF shows a significant lag for 1 month and the PACF shows a significant lag for 1 month, with perhaps some significant lag at 12,24 and 48 months.
